Hounsfield · Development Log

The X-ray machine already reached the village. The radiologist never did.

Why I built an AI that reads chest X-rays entirely on a phone, with no internet connection required — and why that constraint, not a feature list, is the whole point.

Partha•On-device medical AI

Here's a strange fact about global health: an X-ray machine is one of the cheapest, most durable pieces of diagnostic equipment in medicine, and it has made it into clinics in places that have almost nothing else. What hasn't made it there is the person qualified to read the film.

The World Health Organization estimates that roughly two-thirds of the world's population has no access to basic radiology services — not because there's no machine, but because there's no radiologist within reach, no reliable internet to send the image to one, or no time before the patient has already gone home. In many district hospitals and rural clinics, a chest X-ray gets read by whichever clinician is on shift, using judgment built from experience rather than formal radiological training. Most of the time that's good enough. Sometimes — a subtle pneumothorax, an early TB infiltrate, a nodule easy to miss — it isn't, and the cost of that gap falls on exactly the patients who can least absorb it.

That gap is what put Hounsfield on my desk. Not as a startup idea, not as a demo — as a question I couldn't leave alone: what if the interpretation didn't need to travel anywhere at all? What if it lived on the same device that took the photo of the film, running entirely offline, so that "no radiologist within reach" and "no internet" stopped being reasons a patient waits days for an answer?

Why "offline" isn't a limitation — it's the design

Almost every AI health tool I looked at assumes a network connection to a cloud model as a given. That assumption quietly excludes the places I care most about reaching: rural clinics, field hospitals, disaster response, low-connectivity regions where the electricity is more reliable than the data signal. If the tool only works with good bandwidth, it was never really built for the grassroots — it was built for a hospital that already has everything else.

So Hounsfield runs MedGemma — Google's medical vision-language model — directly on the device itself. On iPhone, that means Apple's MLX framework driving the on-chip GPU; in a browser, WebGPU with a WASM fallback. Once the model is on the device, it never needs to leave. No image, no patient context, no report is ever transmitted anywhere unless a clinician explicitly chooses to export a backup. A district clinic with a single shared iPhone and no signal can still capture a chest X-ray and get a structured, on-device read: modality and region, findings, an impression, and a recommendation — in seconds, offline, for free after the one-time setup.

Medical AI should work anywhere, for anyone — even without the internet.

That line has been the north star of every engineering decision since. It's why the model ships bundled inside the app rather than depending on a download that might stall in a place with poor connectivity. It's why every study, image, and report is encrypted at rest and gated behind Face ID or Touch ID — a tool meant for real clinical use has to take patient privacy as seriously as any hospital system would, especially with no IT department backing it up. It's why re-analysis lets a clinician correct the context and try again rather than trusting a single pass blindly. None of these are headline features. They're the difference between a demo and something a health worker could actually trust with a real patient.

What's actually running under the hood

  • MedGemma 1.5 4B, Google's medically-tuned vision-language model, doing the interpretation
  • Fully on-device inference — Apple MLX natively on iPhone, WebGPU/WASM in the browser
  • Zero network requirement after the one-time setup — works in airplane mode
  • AES-encrypted local storage, unlocked with Face ID / Touch ID
  • Structured reports: Technique, Findings, Impression, Recommendations — every time
  • Editable clinical context so a re-read isn't just a repeat of the first guess
To be exactly clear

Hounsfield is not a diagnostic device and doesn't claim to be one. Every report carries that disclaimer, and it means it: this is a second pair of eyes, a triage aid, a way to flag "this probably needs a closer look" in a place where the alternative isn't a second opinion — it's no opinion at all. The goal is narrowing that gap, not pretending it doesn't require a physician's judgment.

Where this goes next

The version running today reads chest X-rays and CT slices, on a phone, offline. That's the floor, not the ceiling. The road ahead includes broader imaging modalities, tighter clinical validation, and — the part I think about most — getting this into the hands of the clinics that inspired it in the first place, not just the App Store browsers who'll never need it out of necessity.

If any part of this resonates — if you work in global health, rural medicine, radiology, or you've stood in a clinic wishing the machine in the corner could tell you more than it did — I'd genuinely like to hear from you. This project gets better with exactly that kind of contact with the real problem.
